My second-favourite movie of all time is Bong Joon-Ho’s second feature, Memories of Murder (first place is yet to be taken by The Matrix, and that movie will have its separate post soon). Koreans decided that is it in fact the best Korean movie of all time, and I couldn’t agree more, even if the competition is fierce (I love Korean cinema much more than the American one, and I’m still amazed how much quality they can output on such small budgets).

Based on a play and actual events that took place in Korea in the 80s, the film revolves around a team of mostly inexperienced investigators trying to catch a serial killer in rural Korea. While the usual tropes and plot points of a serial killer picture are all there, what sets Memories apart is the flawless direction and execution. From the cinematography, acting, score, set design, sound mixing, etc, to all the little choices made to tell the story and realise the director’s vision, you can tell that Memories of Murder is a movie with no *accidental* good things in it. There’s no Bob Ross *happy accidents* shit going on here, everything is meticulously and deliberately created, everything has a purpose. [Read more here from the Criterion article on how much of a perfectionist Director Bong is.](https://www.criterion.com/current/posts/7362-10-things-i-learned-memories-of-murder)

Much like Zodiac, a David Fincher picture done in much the same vein, four years later (and a fantastic picture in its own right), Director Bong decided to never tell us who the real killer is, up until the end. There are various suspects over the course of the film, and with each reveal, when the killer shows up on screen in blink-and-you’ll-miss glimpses, he is portrayed by the actor of the current suspect, keeping in line with eyewitness testimonies. This is a clever choice, but Director Bong goes one step further with his fabulous ending which, like all my favourite things, I didn’t quite like at first.

 [![Memories of Murder Ending HD](https://img.youtube.com/vi/yJc9O8ogyuM/0.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yJc9O8ogyuM)

Like the opening, it is the only fully saturated scene in the film (I haven’t watched the remastered version with the darker, newer color grade, but judging from the screenshots I can’t help but think that Director Bong and the DP George Lucas’d their film). What strikes me, though, is the final shot of the film, when Song Kang-ho’s character looks straight into the camera, breaking the fourth wall. While I initially considered the shot as an attempt at a pretentious *oh, it could’ve been you, or any one of us* message, that choice is actually WAY more clever (which goes to show why Bong is a multiple Oscar winner and I’m a schmuck, haha :) ). That shot was meant *FOR THE KILLER*. Yes, in 2003, way before [the killer, already serving a sentence for an unrelated murder, confessed to his crimes in 2019.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Memories_of_Murder#Real_life_case). But nobody could’ve guessed that, and, by all intents and purposed, the killer was roaming free through Korea, very likely still alive, and with the god-like ego of a man that escaped justice. It was extremely likely that he would’ve went to see the film about himself (much like the Zodiac killer did during his time), and that forth-wall breaking gaze was meant to meet his, as if the director was looking him in the eye directly. When I read that, my mind exploded. What a brilliant way to utilise art! Truly the work of a man in complete control of his craft.

I love Parasite, The Host, Mother, Barking dogs never bite and pretty much all of Bong’s filmography (I’m so-so on his English features, but they still contain ingots of brilliance, and I suspect the language barrier to get in the way between him and all actors not named Tilda Swinton).

I loved that ending so much, I tried to morph it with that *You talkin to me?* scene from Taxi Driver in this panel from Millennium. (I don’t know how well I even communicated that Han was talking in the mirror, but hey… and attempt was made).
