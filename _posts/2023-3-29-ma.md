Have you ever noticed how your brain automatically tunes out a loud AC in the background, or the jackhammer down the street, or even a horrible toothache? That's because the brain is (in most cases) smart. It is, after all, the only organ that named itself. It is so smart that it knows to stop focusing on constant stimuli since no new information is being received. We'll be coming back to this point.

In September of 2002, famed movie critic Roger Ebert had an interview with Hayao Miyazaki, who next year was going to win an Oscar for Spirited Away. Here's an excerpt from the interview:

*I told Miyazaki I love the "gratuitous motion" in his films; instead of every movement being dictated by the story, sometimes people will just sit for a moment, or they will sigh, or look in a running stream, or do something extra, not to advance the story but only to give the sense of time and place and who they are.
"We have a word for that in Japanese," he said. "It's called ma. Emptiness. It's there intentionally."*
*Is that like the "pillow words" that separate phrases in Japanese poetry?*
*"I don't think it's like the pillow word." He clapped his hands three or four times. "The time in between my clapping is ma. If you just have non-stop action with no breathing space at all, it's just busyness, But if you take a moment, then the tension building in the film can grow into a wider dimension. If you just have constant tension at 80 degrees all the time you just get numb."*

The first time I felt like an old man feeling left behind I was watching Man of Steel, the Zack Snyder superhero film about Superman. There were a lot of instances of Ma in that film, but 90% were artificially created by me, pausing the movie to rub my temples. The rest 10% were scenes with Diane Lane playing Superman’s mom. I felt like David Lynch being driven by George Lucas to the Skywalker Ranch, and I couldn't figure out why. I was into seeing the movie, still on the early 2010s Christopher Nolan hype train, and I generally liked Snyder’s visual style, as shallow as his use of it tended to be. Later, I simply realized that the movie exhausted me, to the point where the screen melted into the wall behind it. I don't watch movies to relax, but it was the first time I felt the need for a hot bath after a superhero story.

Man of Steel failed to provide contrast. By keeping the action almost constantly dialed up to 11, nothing packed a punch anymore, because every action Superman took implied the demolition of a building and/or a massive orange explosion as if people would get bored if that didn't happen for 5 seconds. Some would blame it on modern movies or modern audiences, but that's a bit unfair. Filmmakers like David Fincher, Park Chan Wook or Bong Joon Ho have a lot of stilted moments in their films, yet they keep delivering that electric tension that keeps thousands glued to the screen for the entirety of the movie. They keep it interesting because they provide contrast.
