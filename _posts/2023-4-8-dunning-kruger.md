Is there something worse than being a complete brain dead, eat-glue-in-arts-and-crafts, stupid idiot?

Yes. It’s being average.

Nobody cares for the averages, the mids, the quiet ones, the faded, the whatever is cool to call average in your current vernacular. People tell you that it’s nice not to stick out, but if you do, to do it in a proactive way, to excel, to be an achiever, an overachiever if possible.

But why? If you’re an idiot, you are *the idiot*, if you’re the genius you are *the genius*, but what about everything in between? Well, you are in the back row of a group photo, probably cut by someone’s shoulder. You are the B’s student that never caused any trouble, but your teacher will struggle to remember you when you bump into her at the supermarket. You are one of the “what’s-his-face”s of the world, the “oh, him/her!” of every group, someone who made an impression just by how little of a presence he or she had. A footnote.

I used to think I was a sharp kid. Smart. I was a big fish in a small pond, living in a ghetto-ass town that my parents settled in, nerdy beyond belief and unable to fit in, and I was *smart* for that place. And with how little I had going for myself at that point, being smart way my one victory and reason of pride in the world. It felt very important for me to be smart, and more importantly, to be *smarter* that everyone else. This played no small part in my developing repelling personality, which fed a toxic feedback system of getting more jaded and more in need of proving *the idiots* wrong. The teachers played their part in this, too, encouraging me to stick to the books and keep being the star student (except for P.E., obviously). See, I didn’t realise at the time, but the only subject I genuinely enjoyed for the joy of learning and practicing was art, but I was already on a path for a life, both academic and professional, in the computer science field.

Now, the problem with getting told over and over how smart you are is that it disconnects the effort of learning from the reward of performing. I stopped putting in the effort, coasting on my *smartness*, and the signs of my decline were already showing, but I did not notice. The notion of being smart was way too reinforced for me to accept anything else.

![Dunning Kruger](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_Effect_01.svg/512px-Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_Effect_01.svg.png)

Then, I moved schools, then high school came around, then college (both bachelors and masters, somehow, in no small part due to the benefit of remote classes during the COVID-19 pandemic). And at each step, trading the small pond for a bigger and bigger one and putting less and less effort each time, the cruel reality started to sink in. At first, it was a shock, the shock of being an idiot, but finally, a crueler realisation was established: I was average, all along.

Middle of the road.

Mid.

Not even stupid enough to remember.

It just so happened that I was putting in some extra effort early on and was quite good at remembering random trivia (which  helped me in writing, the only field where that would be useful except game shows). I was never smart, and I was never memorable, and that hurt. My *I’m an idiot* phase was my low point on the Dunning-Kruger curve, before my auto evaluation and my actual competence lines started to overlap.

Now I’m content to who I am. I’m glad I’m not an idiot, and I’m at piece with being a background figure in other people’s lives. It would have been nice if people from my formative years did not hype me up so much and set me up for disappointment, but I can’t blame them too much, though. Everybody is excited at the prospect of developing a prodigy, so much so that they forget that people develop at different rates. 

More often than not, hard work beats talent. I’d go as far as to say that there is no such thing as talent; it is an umbrella term for hard work, passion and experience.

I had some embarrassing moment in my life where I carried myself with the confidence of a genius, only to fall flat on my face when push came to shove. Now I know better, and I started trying again. This time, though, I’ll know what was the reason when I’ll succeed.
