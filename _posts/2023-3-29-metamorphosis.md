I hate cockroaches. I used to keep onions in the sink cupboard of my one-bedroom apartment, and apparently, it was the cause for the little fuckers showing up all the time. At least I think it was because they didn't show up quite so often after that (I'm still in the habit of leaving dirty dishes in the sink to this day). Point is, there was a lot of stomping and scrubbing involved, and the bigger they were, the nastier it got.

But why do we hate cockroaches? They're quite resilient, both from what I read and saw with my own eyes (you know the factoids: cut their head and they stay alive, they can survive q nuclear holocaust, yada-yada). They just want to live, same as any of us.

Well, might want to live, but society has deemed them undesirable, and in my apartment, I was society. The little brown bastards looked nasty, crawling around all surfaces with unpredictable and jerky moves. What's more, they lived at my place but PAID NO RENT!!! Now, what kind of homeowner would I be if I didn't dream about being a landlord? Problem is, even if the cockroach wants to pay, he has no money, and the cockroach has no money because the cockroach has no job. See where this is going?

Kafka published Metamorphosis in 1915 in the October issue of the journal Die weißen Blätter. It was one of the few works that he managed to finish and publish during his lifetime. I myself see a lot of myself in Kafka. Well, not in the ‘astronomical talent for writing and dark humor’ department, more in the ‘reject of society that does a bullshit job and dreams of turning writing into the main job, even though I'll probably never finish anything and write in my will for all that crap to be deleted’ department. I can also see how Kafka saw himself in Gregor Samsa, his character, because he is a Twilight Zone version of himself.

Gregor doesn't feel at home in society and has that people-pleaser attitude that a bullied kid adopts: don't be confrontational (which often he lumps together with being assertive and standing your ground when it's fair), keep the head low, say ‘sorry’ all the time, make excuses, don't raise the voice, everyone else is scarier and better at this ‘being alive’ business so any resistance is futile. It's quite uncomfortable seeing how well Kafka depicts that attitude in Gregor, a character I would've reading hated as a kid, had I not watched Evangelion (more on that in a future post).

What form does Gregor take when he reaches the zenith of that feeling of no self-worth? Yes, he wakes up as a cockroach (or at least as something similar looking, going by the description in the text). A cockroach is liked by nobody, it cannot do the job that Gregor does, board the train on time, or help around the house. Feeling like a human family gave birth to a cockroach could also be the feeling of alienation when one doesn't feel like belonging even with his next of kin. A cockroach is not physically attractive (often the main factor by which people decide who to like), it is not charming and it is not funny (unlike the text, surprisingly; when Gregor crawled up on the wall to protect his precious photo frame from his sister and saying in inner monologue that WELL, SHE CAN TRY TO TAKE IT in his one moment of assertiveness, I felt my sides bursting). A cockroach is nasty and, by all means, should just die, for its existence alone brings discomfort to the fine people around. But the cockroach, like all living things, doesn't want to die, and why should the cockroach die? It has a right to live, like everyone else!

I read Metamorphosis in a very literal way on story level; there's no doubt that those things happened, they are part of the absurd that makes the story funny and bittersweet. But I was left with my own conclusion afterward: I get it, Gregor, and I get it, Kafka. I often feel the same. I wasn't blessed with a symmetrical, handsome face, and years of bullying from classmates and control from a narcissistic parent left me with little self-worth and an abrasive personality that people hate. I often feel like the biggest cockroach in my apartment, and all the little guys just come to pay their respects, inevitably getting stomped because I can't accept my position as King Roach.

But I have a right to live, even if others say otherwise. I won't disappear like Gregor at the end of the story. That's the pits of despair. And yeah, unsurprisingly, everyone else felt relief when that happened. They cried, in that hypocritical way, but it was more if a cry for their own pain and suffering. Death is a tragedy, unless you're a cockroach.

But the cockroach deserves to live, too! 🪳
